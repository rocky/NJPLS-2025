But you can't. And that's a big part of why I am here.

I could have included at least two dozen more books. I also looked at the all of the class lecture notes people mentioned in the survey. None of those appear to cover the topic of this talk either.

I have here some books that are out of print. But this just shows that this silence on the topic is not new. I also have some rather specialized books, such as one on Pascal compilers, or advanced compiler writing. But you won't find a specialized compiler book that includes information on debugging support and debuggers.

It should come as no surprise then, that if the popular books on compilers are silent about debuggers and debugging support, then the compilers that people write don't have debuggers either.

And this is confirmed in the survey. Over 90% of the people who have said they were part of a compiler team, report that the compiler that they worked on had no debugger.

These are two books, that do describe debuggers as part of the compiler. But the topic is specific for the compiler described in the book. And I wonder how many people are familiar with either of these. [I won't ask for a survey here.]

The prevailing view found in compiler books and debugger books, is that these are separate topics.

This reminds me of a couple of places where I worked, where we had separate departments for computer backups, and for computer restores.

The first group, backup software, was group to work in — the atomosphere was causal and relaxed.

In contrast, the department-of-restores group was always under constant pressure and never could get its act together.

It feels to me that this is, too often, the current situation with compilers and debuggers.
